On "The Problems of Philosophy"

I finished reading reading Bertrand Russell's "The Problem's of Philosophy". For a rather slim book, it took an incredibly long time to read. This makes sense. While the book was short, it certainly was dense. Russell's work features sentences the size of paragraphs and moves quickly through abstract ideas. It wasn't exactly jumping into the deep end of the pool of philosophy nor was the book shallow. The book hit a good blend of the two where the final product was comprehensible to a newcomer in the field, but only after some work. Essentially, each paragraph either presented a new argument or criticized a presented argument. The result being that losing focus while reading makes it impossible to understand what the book is saying. Russell starts his readers off by telling the story of Descartes and his arrival at "je pense, donc je suis". We then look at the validity of information arriving through our senses and from there begin a trek into considering the nature of that information. The book ends by considering what the limits of knowledge are and then an argument for why one would study philosophy. This last chapter was my favorite. Russell's argument was that philosophy opens the mind to new possibilities, and that the questions that are being asked are just as interesting as answers that may or may not be extant. It was the hardest book I read while in college, but it may have been the most worthwhile.
